Section 3.1: Second-Degree Stochastic Dominance (SSD)

Core Idea

  • Asset A must offer at least the same expected return as Asset B
  • But with less risk (measured by variance)

This leads us to portfolio selection: among all portfolios offering the same expected return, the dominant one is the one with the least variance.

What Is Second-Degree Stochastic Dominance (SSD)?

SSD is a decision rule for risk-averse investors.

Let \( F_A(x) \) and \( F_B(x) \) be cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of the returns of assets A and B.

We say A dominates B in the second degree if:

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{x} F_A(t)\, dt \leq \int_{-\infty}^{x} F_B(t)\, dt \quad \text{for all } x \]

...and strictly less for at least one \( x \). This reflects preference under concave utility.

Interpretation

  • For risk-averse agents, less variance is better, holding expected return constant.
  • If Asset A has equal mean but lower variance, then it’s preferred under SSD.

In Portfolio Context

Suppose you have many portfolios, all yielding \( E[R] = 8\% \).

SSD says: pick the one with the lowest variance — that’s the efficient frontier logic in Markowitz’s framework.

Section 3.2: Mean-Variance Model and Investor Preferences

This points to the Markowitz (1952) model — the birth of Modern Portfolio Theory.

Assumptions

  • Investors are rational and risk-averse
  • Their utility depends only on mean and variance of return
  • Monotonicity: Higher return is preferred
  • Strict concavity: Diminishing marginal utility (risk aversion)

Implication

Utility increases with expected return but decreases with variance. So, when choosing between portfolios:

\[ \text{Investor wants:} \quad \text{High } E[\tilde{R}] \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Low } \text{Var}(\tilde{R}) \]

Section 3.3: Utility Function Expansion Using Taylor Series

This section bridges economics and math. It shows how utility can be approximated using Taylor expansion around the expected wealth.

Given

Let \( \tilde{W} \) be the random end-of-period wealth. Utility is expanded as:

\[ u(\tilde{W}) = u(E[\tilde{W}]) + u'(E[\tilde{W}])(\tilde{W} - E[\tilde{W}]) + \frac{1}{2}u''(E[\tilde{W}])(\tilde{W} - E[\tilde{W}])^2 + R_3 \]

Taking Expectations

\[ E[u(\tilde{W})] = u(E[\tilde{W}]) + \frac{1}{2}u''(E[\tilde{W}])\sigma^2(\tilde{W}) + E[R_3] \]

What’s Going On?

  • \( u(E[\tilde{W}]) \): Base utility from expected wealth
  • \( \frac{1}{2}u''(E[\tilde{W}])\sigma^2(\tilde{W}) \): Penalty for variance — since \( u'' < 0 \), this lowers utility
  • \( E[R_3] \): Correction using higher-order moments (skewness, kurtosis, etc.)

Why Stop at Second Order?

  • In mean-variance analysis, we assume the remainder is negligible
  • Real-world utility might depend on skewness (e.g., preference for upside risk)
  • Expected utility isn’t fully captured by just \( E[\tilde{W}] \) and \( \text{Var}(\tilde{W}) \)

Notation Breakdown

  • \( u^{(n)}(E[\tilde{W}]) \): n-th derivative of utility at mean wealth
  • \( m^{(n)}(\tilde{W}) = E[(\tilde{W} - E[\tilde{W}])^n] \): n-th central moment

So:

\[ E[R_3] = \sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} u^{(n)}(E[\tilde{W}]) m^{(n)}(\tilde{W}) \]

Intuition

  • Skewness (3rd moment): Measures asymmetry. Positive skew = upside potential
  • Kurtosis (4th moment): Measures fat tails or extreme events

So even if two assets have same mean and variance, one might be preferred due to positive skew — this is not captured in Markowitz's model.

Summary of Key Insights

  • SSD: Prefers lower variance at equal expected return; relates to concave utility
  • Mean-Variance: Simplified model: optimize return vs risk; assumes utility is quadratic or normal distribution
  • Taylor Expansion: Expresses expected utility using moments of distribution
  • Remainder Term: Shows higher moments (skewness, kurtosis) matter for full utility characterization

Final Thought

This section lays the theoretical foundation for why we care about mean and variance in asset allocation — but also hints that reality is richer. Especially when dealing with real markets (with non-normal distributions), ignoring higher moments can misrepresent investor preferences.
